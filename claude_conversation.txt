What you should see, in order:
1. quies=3045 qrels=3045 ... — confirms metadata files loaded
2. Sampled 150 text-only queries (seed=42) — deterministic, so the same seed always picks the same queries
3. They reference ~50 unique PDFs — the count is whatever falls out of the sample; usually ~30–60
4. PDF download progress (this is the slowest part — arxiv has rate limits; the 0.4s sleep between requests is intentional)
5. Ingestion progress — each PDF becomes ~10–50 chunks via your 500-word chunker
6. Mapping summary with overlap statistics — this is where you learn whether the threshold is right

The number to watch most carefully: dropped (no chunk crossed threshold). If it's >30% of your sample, the threshold is too strict — chunks aren't matching gold sections, probably because your chunker's boundaries don't align with the dataset's section boundaries. Try --threshold 0.20 and re-run with --skip-download --skip-ingest.

If median overlap fraction is very low (say <0.1), the dataset's section text and your extracted PDF tan expected — possibly a PDF extraction quality issue worth investigating.

**Why this happens:** macOS keeps its trusted CAs in the system Keychain. Python's `urllib` doesn't read the Keychain — it relies on an OpenSSL CA bundle that's only present on macOS if (a) you ran `Install Certificates.command` after installing python.org's Python, or (b) you explicitly point at one with `SSL_CERT_FILE`. The `certifi` package ships Mozilla's CA bundle, which is the standard workaround.

## Part 2: Does chunking section text actually test your chunker?

**Yes — and arguably it tests it *better* than the PDF approach. Here's why.**

A chunker's job is: *given a document of text, split it into pieces such that question-relevant content stays together in a single piece*. To evaluate that job, you need to ask: **"Given this document, does my chunker keep the answer-relevant text intact in some chunk?"**

When you test on PDFs, you're actually evaluating **two systems chained together**:

```
PDF → [PyPDF2 extraction] → [your chunker] → chunks
        ^^^^^^^^^^^^^^^^^^^   ^^^^^^^^^^^^^
        unknown quality       what you want to measure
```

If your chunks miss the gold section text, it could be because:
- (a) PyPDF2 mangled the text so your chunks contain different words than the gold section → not your chunker's fault, and
- (b) Your chunker cut the answer across a chunk boundary → actually your chunker's fault.

You can't tell which. That's the situation you're in now: **median overlap = 0.000 doesn't tell you anything about your chunker, because the text fed into your chunker doesn't even contain the same words as the gold section.**

When you test on **section text**, the pipeline becomes:

```
clean section text → [your chunker] → chunks
                     ^^^^^^^^^^^^^
                     isolated, the only variable
```

Now if a gold section doesn't appear in a chunk, it's because **your chunker decided to put the boundary in a bad place** — and that's exactly the diagnostic signal you want.

Two questions in one — let me handle them separately. The PDF extraction question is purely technical (and important to understand), and the embedding question has a small misconception I want to clear up.

## Part 1: How exactly does PyPDF2 fail?

The fundamental issue: **PDFs are not a text format. They're a visual layout format.**

A PDF doesn't store "this is paragraph 3 of section 2." It stores instructions like "draw the glyph 'a' at coordinates (213.4, 612.8) on page 4." There's no inherent concept of words, paragraphs, columns, or reading order. Extracting text means *reverse-engineering* the layout — and that's where things go wrong. Concrete failure modes:

| Failure mode | What goes in | What PyPDF2 returns |
|---|---|---|
| **Reading order in 2-column papers** | "Method We use…" (left col) followed by "Results We found…" (right col) | Often interleaves columns — line 1 of left, line 1 of right, line 2 of left… → soup |
| **Word boundaries** | "For example, we use…" | Sometimes `"Forexample,weuse"` because PDFs don't always emit explicit spaces — PyPDF2 infers them from x-coordinates and gets it wrong |
| **Hyphenation at line ends** | "exam-\nple" (line break inside a word) | `"exam- ple"` or `"examample"` instead of joining to `"example"` |
| **Ligatures** | "ﬁgure" (the `ﬁ` glyph is one character in the font) | Sometimes becomes `"gure"` or a box `"□gure"` because the glyph maps weirdly |
| **Math/formulas** | $\alpha + \beta = \gamma$ | Garbled symbols, missing entirely, or random Unicode escapes |
| **Headers/footers interleaved** | body, body, *"Page 3"* (footer), body, body | `"…the result is significant. Page 3 We then computed…"` — page numbers smeared into prose |
| **Tables** | A formatted table | A flat string of cell contents in some order, with no structure |
| **Special characters** | em-dash, Greek letters, smart quotes | `"?"`, `"\xc3\xa9"`, or replaced with random ASCII |

You can see this concretely if you want — I can run PyPDF2 on one of your already-downloaded arxiv PDFs and put it side-by-side with the same paper's corpus JSON. Let me know if you'd like that.

**PyPDF2 specifically is one of the *worse* extractors.** The industry-better options are `pymupdf` (a.k.a. fitz), `pdfplumber`, and `pdfminer.six`. For arxiv papers specifically, the *best* extraction comes from the **LaTeX source** the authors submitted alongside the PDF — and that's almost certainly what the RAGBench authors used to build the corpus JSONs. That's why their text is *Markdown-clean*: it was extracted from LaTeX, not from rendered PDFs.

So when you ingest from PDFs, you're racing against a competitor (the dataset) who started with the same papers but had access to the LaTeX source code. You can't win that race with PyPDF2.

## Part 2: The embedding misconception (small but important to fix)

You said:
> "this selection text is more question-answer style, which makes it much easier for embeddings to understand what should be answered for what"

**This isn't quite right — let me correct it carefully because it matters.**

The corpus JSON's section text is **not** Q-A style. It's the same expository research-paper prose that's in the PDF. Same words, same paragraphs, same meaning. The only difference is *extraction quality* (clean vs. noisy bytes), not *content style*. There are no questions in the corpus JSON; just the body of the paper.

So why does embeddings work better on it? Two real reasons, neither related to "style":

1. **Cleaner input = better embeddings.** A 1024-dim embedding tries to capture semantic meaning. If your input is `"Forexample,wetestedthemodelon… ﬁgure 3"`, the embedder sees garbage tokens and produces a less precise vector. Same paragraph cleaned up — `"For example, we tested the model on… Figure 3"` — produces a sharper, more retrievable vector. This is purely about input quality, not Q-A structure.

2. **Alignment with the ground truth.** RAGBench's gold sections were defined *over the clean text*. When your chunks come from clean text, your chunks contain literally the same characters as the gold. That makes both (a) the n-gram overlap mapping work and (b) embedding-based retrieval more likely to score gold sections well, because the chunk's vector is computed from the *same* tokens the gold answer came from.

**What you got right**: clean text → better embeddings. Correct.
**What was off**: the cleanness isn't about Q-A style. The corpus has no questions. It's the same paper prose, just better-extracted.

A useful way to think about it: **embeddings don't care about style; they care about signal-to-noise.** Whether the underlying text is a question, a research paragraph, or a recipe — the embedder maps its *meaning* to a vector. Garbled input → garbled meaning → garbled vector → bad retrieval. That's it.

## First, the median=0 is expected (not bad)

That number is the median of `overlap_stats`, which is appended *for every (chunk, query) pair* — not per-query. So for each query, the script computes the overlap of *all* chunks in that doc against the gold section. A typical doc has ~20–30 chunks, only 1–2 of which match the gold section. The other 20+ chunks come from completely different sections and have ~0 overlap *by design*. Median across all those pairs = 0 is mechanical, not informative.

The **useful** signals are:
- ✅ Max = 1.000 (was 0.945) — chunks now match sections word-for-word when they line up. Confirms text-source switch worked.

## Why are 40 queries still dropping? — Hypothesis

I'm pretty sure I know. Look at how `overlap_fraction` is computed:

```python
return len(chunk_grams & section_grams) / len(chunk_grams)
```

The denominator is **chunk n-grams**, not section n-grams. That metric asks: *"What fraction of this chunk is from the gold section?"*

The problem: if the gold section is **short** (say 30 words) and the chunk is your usual 500 words, then:
- `section_grams` ≈ 23
- `chunk_grams` ≈ 493
- Even if the chunk fully contains the section, the score is 23 / 493 ≈ **0.05** — below the 0.3 threshold.

So you're systematically dropping queries whose gold section is shorter than ~150 words, even though their full content is captured by a chunk. That's not a chunker problem — it's a *metric* problem.

## The right metric for "is this chunk gold for this query?"

For RAG eval, a chunk is "gold" if retrieving it would *expose the answer*. The right question is the reverse: *"What fraction of the gold section is captured by this chunk?"*

```python
return len(chunk_grams & section_grams) / len(section_grams)
```

Now:
- Short section (23 grams) fully in chunk → 23/23 = **1.0** ✅
- Long section (993 grams) split across two chunks of ~496 grams each → each chunk scores ~0.50 (both correctly flagged as gold) ✅
- Chunk from an unrelated section → ~0 (correctly rejected) ✅

This metric is symmetric to the *real* concern: does this chunk contain enough of the gold to answer the query.
